## Introduction

TikTok has a lead generation problem that most brands haven't solved yet.

The platform drives enormous purchase intent. Someone watches a video about a skincare routine and immediately goes to the comments to ask "where can I get that moisturiser?" A fitness influencer posts about their programme and 200 people comment asking for the link. A DTC brand goes viral and the comment section fills with "how much does this cost?" and "do you ship to Canada?"

These are buyers. They're announcing their intent publicly, in writing, in real time.

The problem is that most brands miss them. Comments scroll by faster than any team can manually monitor. The high-intent questions get buried under generic reactions within hours. By the time a sales or marketing person checks the comments — if they check at all — the buyer has already moved on.

This guide covers how to find those leads systematically: what signals to look for, where to look, how to move fast enough, and what to do when you find them.

---

## Why TikTok Comments Specifically?

Three things make TikTok comments particularly valuable for lead generation compared to other social platforms:

**1. Purchase intent is explicit and impulsive.** TikTok drives a category of behaviour marketers call "discovery purchasing" — someone sees something, wants it immediately, and goes to the comments to figure out how to get it. This doesn't happen at the same rate on LinkedIn or Twitter, where content consumption is more passive.

**2. Comments are public and searchable.** Unlike DMs or stories, TikTok comments are fully public. You can read them, analyse them, and respond to them without any algorithmic gatekeeping. The person who commented "where do I buy this" is publicly visible to anyone watching the same video.

**3. The competition isn't paying attention.** Most brands still treat TikTok comments as a reputation management channel — something to monitor for complaints. The proportion of brands actively mining TikTok comments for purchase intent is still very small. The gap between the volume of buying signals and the teams actively responding to them is large.

## What High-Intent Comments Look Like

Not all comments are leads. Before building any monitoring or outreach system, you need to know what you're looking for.

High-intent signals fall into several categories:

**Direct purchase questions:**
- "Where can I buy this?"
- "How much does this cost?"
- "Is this available in the UK?"
- "Do you ship to [country]?"
- "What's the website?"
- "Link??"

**Product comparison questions:**
- "How is this different from [competitor]?"
- "Is this better than [alternative product]?"
- "Which one should I get?"

**Problem-acknowledging comments:**
- "I've been looking for something like this for ages"
- "This is literally what I need"
- "Taking notes on this"
- "Sending this to my [partner/friend/boss]"

**Referral and tag comments:**
- "@[friend] this is literally you"
- "@[friend] we need this"

The last category is particularly valuable: a person tagging a friend is creating a lead *and* a warm referral simultaneously. The tagged person has been pre-sold by someone they trust.

**Lower-intent signals to deprioritise:**
- Generic praise ("love this!", "amazing content")
- Engagement bait responses ("follow for more")
- Off-topic comments

## Where to Look

There are three types of TikTok content to monitor for comment leads:

### 1. Your Own Content

The most obvious source. If you're posting on TikTok and generating views, your comment sections are the first place to check. High-intent comments on your own content are the warmest leads possible — the person has already been exposed to your product or service.

**What to do:** Check comments within the first 2 hours of a post going live (this is peak engagement window), then again at 24 hours. Respond directly to purchase questions with a link, price, or next step — don't leave the question unanswered.

### 2. Competitor Content

If your competitors are active on TikTok, their comment sections are a lead generation goldmine that most brands ignore entirely.

When someone comments "is this better than [your product]?" on a competitor's video, they're comparison shopping. They haven't committed. A well-timed response — or a direct outreach — can bring them across.

**What to do:** Identify your 5-10 main competitors on TikTok. Monitor their most recent 3-5 posts. Look for comparison questions, price questions, and "should I switch?" signals. You can respond publicly to the comment (friendly, not aggressive), or DM the person directly if they've engaged publicly.

### 3. Influencer and Creator Content in Your Category

When influencers review or feature products in your category, their audiences are highly qualified. Someone watching a "best AI tools for coaches" TikTok and asking "which one would you recommend for [specific use case]?" is a precisely targeted lead.

**What to do:** Identify 10-20 creators in your niche who regularly feature products similar to yours. Monitor their content weekly. Focus on videos that discuss the category you compete in.

## The Manual Monitoring Process

If you're doing this without automation tools, here's a workable system:

**Daily (20-30 min):**
1. Check your own recent posts (last 72 hours) for new high-intent comments
2. Check the 3 most recent posts from your top 5 competitors
3. Respond to or log high-intent signals

**Weekly (60-90 min):**
1. Check influencer content from the past week (your list of 10-20 creators)
2. Search TikTok for category keywords to find new viral content you might have missed
3. Review and respond to any logged leads you haven't acted on

This is manageable for a solo founder or small team. The limitation is speed: by the time you do your daily check, some comments are already 12-24 hours old. Purchase intent cools fast.

## How to Respond to High-Intent TikTok Comments

Finding the comment is half the job. What you do next determines whether it converts.

### Responding Publicly (in the comments)

Do this when:
- The comment is on your own content
- The response adds value to other readers watching the same content
- You can answer the question clearly in one line

**What to say:**
- Direct answer to the purchase question: "Available at signalharvester.com — free trial available"
- Answer with a soft hook: "Happy to DM you the link — a few options depending on your use case"

**What to avoid:**
- Corporate-sounding responses ("Thanks for your interest! Please visit our website for more information")
- Leaving the question unanswered (this signals to everyone reading that the brand doesn't engage)
- Overly promotional responses on a competitor's video

### Responding via DM

Do this when:
- The comment is on a competitor's video (responding in comments there looks aggressive)
- The question needs more than one line to answer
- The person seems ready to buy and you want a higher-conversion interaction

**DM template:**

> Hey [name] — saw your comment on [video], I can help with that. We've got [short specific answer to their question]. Happy to send details / walk you through options if useful? No pressure.

Key elements:
- Reference the specific comment so they know why you're reaching out
- Answer the question immediately — don't make them ask again
- Invite, don't push — "happy to" performs better than "click here to"

### Response speed matters

A comment asking "where can I buy this?" has a half-life of about 30-60 minutes. Respond within that window and you're catching a warm lead. Respond 12 hours later and you're following up with someone who has probably already bought elsewhere, forgotten they asked, or moved on.

Speed of response is the single biggest factor in comment-to-lead conversion rates. Build a system that alerts you fast.
